Outreach Playbook · v1 · 2026-05-12

How I book my first 10 Ascend Systems clients

This is the executable companion to the marketing strategy. The strategy tells me why. This tells me what I do Monday morning at 9:00 AM. I run it for 90 days. Goal: first paying customer in 30 days, three by day 60, six to ten by day 90.

The core principles of cold outreach

  • Specificity beats volume. Twenty emails that name a real fact about the company beat 200 form letters. I am one person. I cannot win on volume. I win on knowing the prospect better than the next guy.
  • The goal of every touch is a 20-minute call, not a sale. Stop trying to close in the email. Close on the phone, in person, over coffee.
  • Local is the moat. Every email and DM mentions Charlotte, a Charlotte landmark, or a Charlotte client. If I sound like I could be anyone in any city, I lose to the offshore agency that charges half.
  • Show the work, do not describe it. Link to the City of Charlotte EV dashboard. Link to the Stancil case study. Pictures of real software beat adjectives.
  • Follow up five times. Most deals close on touch three to five. Stopping at email one is leaving the work undone.
  • Every reply gets a same-day response. Speed of reply is the cheapest competitive advantage available to a solo founder.

1. Daily and weekly cadence

I have 5–10 hours a week for outreach. That is the constraint. The schedule below assumes 7 hours, run in 45-minute blocks before client work starts.

Week 1 is different — burn down the warm list before any cold

The first week of this plan is not the steady-state schedule below. Week 1 is 7 hours of warm reactivation only — see Section 3a. 100 personalized messages to past clients, friends, peers, and dormant LinkedIn connections. This produces more pipeline in week 1 than the cold engine will in month 1, and the steady-state schedule kicks in week 2.

That is fine because cold is NOT the lead engine in this plan — warm reactivation, in-person, and content are. Outscraper filter recipe (the fast way to build the cold universe)

Outscraper pulls Google Maps business data — it is the cheapest way to get a clean 500-row list from a noisy universe. It does not give you revenue or founder names directly (those come from an Apollo / LinkedIn enrichment pass). But the Outscraper layer is where I scale list-building from "hours of LinkedIn scrolling" to "one $25 export."

Location: Charlotte, NC with a 30-mile radius (catches Concord, Mooresville, Huntersville, Gastonia, Rock Hill SC, Indian Trail, Matthews, Cornelius). Or pull by county: Mecklenburg + Cabarrus + Iredell + Union + Lincoln + Gaston + York SC.

Query syntax — use categories, not free-text search

Outscraper's Google Maps endpoint takes a query string, but it matters a lot how you shape it. Pair a single category with a single location — that's it. Free-text searches return Google's top-ranked ~20 results, not the universe. Category queries hit Google's internal category index and paginate the full listing.

Rule: one category + one location per query. Bump limit to 500 per pull (Outscraper's per-query max). If your interface exposes coordinates + radius, use 35.2271, -80.8431 with a 30-mile radius — that's tighter than the "Charlotte NC" string match and reliably catches Mooresville, Concord, Huntersville, Gastonia, Rock Hill SC.

Workflow — Outscraper → enrichment → CRM

  1. Pull ~5,000 raw rows across all 30+ category queries above. One query per category, limit 500 each. Set radius = 30 miles around 35.2271, -80.8431 if your interface allows.
  2. Dedupe by phone number. Companies show up in multiple categories (a place might appear under both "plumbing" and "HVAC"). The phone field is more reliable than name for dedup.
  3. Apply review count ≥ 50 filter → ~800 rows left. Highest-signal single filter on the export.
  4. Drop franchise locations (they buy from HQ, not local) and obvious sole-proprietor shops (single review, no website, just a Google Maps pin) → ~400 rows left.
  5. Enrich top 200 through Apollo.io ($49/mo Starter). Returns: revenue band, employee count, founder/owner name + email + LinkedIn URL. This is the layer that turns Google Maps data into outreach-ready leads.
  6. Run each through the qualification rubric below. 6+ points enters the active outreach list.
  7. Import into Ascend CRM: source = outscraper, warmth = cold, industry tag, sequence = seq-a-generic as default.

3. The outreach channels

I run four channels in parallel. Each has a different cost, a different conversion rate, and a different role. Warm reactivation goes first because it converts at 20–50x cold. In-person is the highest-priority channel for net-new buyers. Cold email is the volume engine. LinkedIn is the relationship glue between all of them.

3a. Warm reactivation (do this first, before any cold)

The single highest-leverage outreach hour I will spend this quarter is touching everyone I already know. Warm reactivation converts at 20–50x cold rates because the trust is already built. Builders avoid this because it feels like asking for a favor — that is exactly why it works. Run this BEFORE any cold sequences go live.

Cadence

  • Week 1: 100 warm touches. From a 500-LinkedIn-connection network plus past clients plus friends, this is 4–6 hours of focused work. Send in batches of 10–15, spread across the week so replies stay manageable.
  • Track replies in CRM with tag warmth = warm, source = warm-reactivation. Promote to hot on "yes, let's talk."
  • Follow up at 14 days if no reply: one short line. "Hey [Name], just bumping this — no pressure. If now is not the moment, no worries." Then close the loop.

Expected return

From 100 well-personalized warm touches: ~15 replies, ~3–5 introductions to someone in the ICP, ~1–3 "yes I have a project" responses. That is more pipeline from week one of warm reactivation than the cold list will produce in a month. This is why it goes first.

3b. In-person and referral

Charlotte is a face-to-face town. Every dollar I have made consulting has come from someone who met me in person. The math is unbeatable: one Chamber lunch with 40 owners in the room is 40 first-touches with a 90% open rate and a built-in handshake.

At least one event per week. Non-negotiable. Rotate across the four event types so I am not running into the same 30 people every Tuesday.

Conversation-starter scripts

I never walk up and pitch. I lead with curiosity, find the work, then offer to be useful. Memorized so I don't fumble at the coffee table.

Opening (anyone, anywhere): "Hey, I'm Brian. What do you do?" — then I shut up and listen for 3 minutes. People love talking about their work. I take mental notes for the hook.
Transition (when they ask what I do): "I run a small tech shop called Ascend Systems out of Charlotte. I build custom software and AI tools for local businesses. Last year I built the EV charging dashboard for the City of Charlotte and a multi-tenant ops platform for a property services company in town. Mostly I help owners get off spreadsheets."
Probe (always, regardless of fit): "What's the most annoying manual process in your business right now? The thing that makes you want to throw your laptop?" — Their answer is the proposal.
Close (if there is a hook): "That sounds like something I could probably help with. Want to grab coffee next week? I'll come to you — I just want to see your setup. No charge, no sales pitch, I just like looking at how businesses actually run."

The "5 clients, 2 intros each" framework

I have 8 portfolio clients and one big-name reference (City of Charlotte). I ask 5 of them this exact question, in person or on a 10-minute call:

"I'm growing Ascend Systems and I'm taking on 2–3 new Charlotte clients this quarter. You know my work. Who are the two business owners you know personally that I should talk to? I'll mention you sent me. I won't waste their time."

5 clients × 2 intros = 10 warm introductions. At a 30% conversion to discovery call and a 30% conversion call-to-proposal, that is one signed deal from the existing network alone. Do this in week 1.

3c. Cold email (the volume engine)

Three sequences, sent through Apollo or Smartlead with a warmed-up brian@ascendsystems.ai mailbox plus a backup brian@lighthouse27.com domain to avoid sending too many from a single sender. Sequences are below in Section 4.

Sending rules

  • Max 25 first-touch sends per day per mailbox. Anything more lights up spam filters.
  • Plain text. No tracking pixels. No images. A plain email from a real person is half the deliverability battle.
  • Send Tuesday–Thursday, 7:30–8:30 AM Eastern. Owners check their inbox over coffee before the day fills up.
  • Personalization in line 1 of every email, every time. Not a merge field — a real sentence about the company.
  • Reply-to is my real address. Replies come into the inbox I check hourly.
  • Suppression list runs. If a domain has bounced or someone has said no, it never gets a second sequence.

The weekly LinkedIn post

One short post every Friday morning. 100–200 words. Always one of these four types:

  1. Case study mini-thread. "Last week I shipped X for a Charlotte client. Here's what we built and what it replaced. Pictures."
  2. Opinion on local tech. "Most Charlotte SMBs do not need a CRM. They need a clean spreadsheet and a Zapier. Here's the rule I use."
  3. AI use case for SMBs. A specific, non-hype application of AI that a property manager or law firm could actually run on Monday.
  4. What I'm seeing in the market. Notes from Chamber events. What founders are asking me. No names unless I have permission.

The posts are not for vanity. They are the trust artifact that an owner reads in week 3 when they get my email and search my name.

7. The first call — 20-minute discovery script

Every first call follows the same shape. I do not improvise. Improvisation is how a 20-minute call becomes a 45-minute waste of two people's time.

If unsure: "Let me think on it overnight and I'll send a note tomorrow with my honest take." Get a yes/no to a next step

Key questions I always ask

  • What does the end of a successful year look like for [COMPANY]?
  • What is your team spending time on that you think a computer should do?
  • Who owns this project on your side, day-to-day?
  • How do you usually buy something like this — fixed-price, hourly, retainer?
  • If I built this and it worked, what would you ask me to build next?

Questions I never ask

  • "What's your decision-making process?" (sales-y — they tune out)
  • "Are you the decision-maker?" (insulting — I already know from the research)

8. CRM hygiene

The Ascend CRM at ascendsystems.ai/admin/projects is the source of truth. Everything goes in. If I touched a lead and didn't log it, the touch didn't happen.

What I log after every touch

  • Touch type: email-sent / email-replied / linkedin-dm / linkedin-comment / in-person / phone / referral-intro
  • Touch date (auto)
  • Touch summary: One sentence. What I said, what they said. "Sent A1. No reply." or "Asked about dispatch workflow. He uses three spreadsheets and a whiteboard."
  • Next action + due date: "Follow up Day 3 — already queued in Apollo" or "Send case study URL by Friday."
  • Status change (if any): queued → sequencing → replied → booked → proposal → won/lost/nurture

Lead becomes a deal when…

A lead is promoted from contact to deal when one of these is true:

  1. A discovery call has been booked on the calendar.
  2. They've replied asking for pricing or scope.
  3. An intro call has happened and there's a clear next step.

At the deal stage I add a project record in the CRM with an estimated deal value, target close date, and one of three stages: discovery / proposal / negotiation. I review the deal board every Friday during reply day.

Industry benchmarks for B2B cold outreach: median reply rates of 8–10% (Apollo, Lemlist published medians), positive reply rates of 1–3%. The numbers above are my targets, not my history.

Kill criteria — when I pivot

  1. Day 30, zero discovery calls. The list or the sequences are broken. Stop sending. Spend a full week auditing list quality, rewriting Sequence A, and running 10 manual hyper-personalized sends to test whether the problem is the message or the audience.
  2. Day 60, zero signed clients. Something more structural is off. Either the offering needs sharpening (fractional CTO is too vague), the price is wrong, or the ICP is wrong. Pause outbound for one week and have 5 honest conversations with people in the pipeline who didn't buy.
  3. Day 90, fewer than 3 signed clients. Re-evaluate the whole motion. The playbook is not working at scale. Consider narrowing the ICP to one vertical and rebuilding the list from scratch.

What I will not do

  • Buy a 10,000-row email list and blast it. Lights up the sending domains and burns the brand.
  • Hire a VA in the Philippines to send my LinkedIn DMs. Defeats the entire premise of "owner-operator who shows up in person."
  • Use AI to write personalization that I haven't read with my own eyes. The whole edge is that I am a real Charlotte person who actually read the company website.
  • Skip the weekly Chamber event because I'm tired. The in-person channel is the highest-leverage activity in this playbook.
  • Drop pricing in an email. Pricing happens on the phone, after I see the work.
  • Take on a client outside Charlotte metro in the first 90 days. The whole geographic moat depends on being local.
