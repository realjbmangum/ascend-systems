Ascend Systems — Marketing Strategy

A direct go-to-market plan for a Charlotte-based technology consultancy. Built to land the first paying customer in 30 days and the first $50k of revenue inside 90.

TL;DR

Ascend Systems is a high-leverage tech consultancy serving growing Charlotte SMBs that have outgrown spreadsheets, Zapier hacks, and freelance developers but cannot justify a $300k full-time engineering hire. Brian Mangum has shipped 14+ live products (including a working dashboard for the City of Charlotte and a multi-tenant operations platform for Stancil Services). The market for that buyer exists, knows it has the problem, and is geographically dense in the Charlotte metro. The go-to-market is not paid ads or content velocity. It is showing up in person, leaning on portfolio proof, and writing about the work in public.

What we are betting on

  • Proximity beats reach. Charlotte SMB owners buy from people they have met. The Chamber room, the BNI chapter, and the founders' Slack are worth more than 10,000 LinkedIn impressions.
  • Proof beats pitch. A demo of the live City of Charlotte EV Dashboard and the Stancil Services ops platform closes deals that no deck can.
  • Geographic focus beats vertical focus. Charlotte SMBs across many industries share the same operational pain — outgrowing manual processes. We sell to the city, not to a niche.

The market opportunity

Charlotte is the fastest-growing major metro in the southeast and one of the fastest in the country. The combination of corporate relocations, banking and fintech expansion, and a population that has roughly doubled in the last two decades has produced a long tail of $1M–$50M SMBs that grew up alongside the city. These businesses are profitable, owner-operated, and operationally behind. Most are still running on QuickBooks, Google Sheets, a Zapier account, and one or two SaaS products with a manual reconciliation step in the middle.

The opportunity is the gap between two unappealing alternatives. On one side, an in-house engineer at Charlotte rates costs $150k–$220k loaded — a number that does not pencil for a $5M revenue company that needs maybe 200 hours of real engineering per year. On the other side, the big consultancies (Accenture, Cognizant, Slalom — all of which have major Charlotte presences servicing Bank of America, Truist, Lowe's, Honeywell, Duke Energy) will not return your call unless the engagement starts at six figures. In between is a vacuum filled badly by Upwork freelancers, white-label GoHighLevel "automation agencies," and Fiverr.

Three forces make right now the right time:

  1. AI compresses what custom software costs to build. A working CRM that used to take six months can now ship in six weeks. The economics of custom software changed in the last 18 months, and most SMB owners have not noticed yet. There is a real, durable window to be the consultancy that explains this to them.
  2. SMB owners are AI-curious but burned by AI snake oil. Every owner has been pitched an "AI tool" by a sales rep who could not answer a single technical question. A local operator who can actually integrate Anthropic and ship working software has scarcity value.
  3. Charlotte's growth means new businesses are forming faster than the existing services ecosystem can serve them. Property managers, multi-location service businesses, and growing trade companies are hitting the operational ceiling every month.

Ideal Customer Profile

The ICP is defined geographically, not vertically. A growing $8M property management company in Matthews and a growing $8M commercial HVAC company in Concord have more in common than either has with their out-of-state peers: same labor market, same digital maturity, same buying motion, often the same Chamber and BNI rooms.

The hard filter

  • Revenue: $1M–$50M annually. Below $1M they cannot afford a $10k engagement; above $50M they have either built an in-house team or signed with a Big-3 consultancy.
  • Location: Headquartered in Mecklenburg, Union, Cabarrus, Iredell, Lincoln, Gaston, or York (SC) counties. Close enough to meet face-to-face inside an hour's drive.
  • Decision-maker: A founder, owner-operator, or general manager with budget authority. No procurement committees, no RFP processes.
  • Operational signal: Currently running on spreadsheets, Zapier, and 4+ disconnected SaaS tools. The owner can name the problem in one sentence.
  • Growth posture: The business is growing. Pain is escalating, not steady. They are hiring, opening new locations, or trying to.

Red flags — who we do not sell to

  • One-truck operators under $1M revenue. They want $200/mo "AI receptionists," not consulting.
  • Companies that have already hired a CTO. The deal is gone; we are the wrong shape.
  • Anyone who needs to pass it through a procurement committee. Sales cycle kills the economics.
  • "Idea-stage" founders with no revenue. Wrong buyer; they need a co-founder, not a consultant.
  • Anyone explicitly shopping for the lowest bidder. We compete on outcomes, not hourly rate.

Channel strategy — ranked by effort vs. payoff

The strategy is bottom-up, warm-first, and multipronged. Cold email alone produces a 1–3% reply rate — at our bandwidth (5–10 hr/wk) that is not enough to hit "first deal in 30 days." So we lead with the channels that convert at 20–50x cold rates (the people who already trust us) and use cold outreach only at high-signal, highly-personalized volume. Content is the moat that makes every other channel work better.

Channel 1 — Warm reactivation

Before any cold outreach happens, every person Brian has worked with, met at an event, or connected with on LinkedIn in the last 24 months gets a touch. This is the single highest-leverage hour in the whole plan.

Who is on the list

  • Past clients. Stancil Services, City of Charlotte contact, Mike (Masonry Contract Review), any LoveNotes / SCDMV / RecordStops users who reached out, anyone who has paid Brian a dollar.
  • Past collaborators. Co-founders, ex-colleagues from prior jobs, consultants and devs Brian has worked alongside, partners on past builds (Oliver / Heirloom).
  • Friends and family who own or run businesses. Anyone in Brian's personal network who fits the ICP (Charlotte SMB owner-operator) or who knows people who do.
  • Dormant LinkedIn connections. Anyone in the 500+ existing LinkedIn network who is a Charlotte SMB founder/operator and has not been touched in 12+ months.

What to send

One short, specific message per person. Never a mass-broadcast. The script:

"Hey [Name] — quick update: I've turned the consulting work into a focused practice serving Charlotte SMBs. Custom CRMs, AI integrations, the kind of automation that takes weeks of manual work off your team. Two questions: (1) is there anything in your business that's been on the 'I should fix this' list for too long? Happy to take a look free. (2) Do you know one Charlotte business owner I should meet? — Brian"

Target: 100 warm touches in week 1. From a 500-LinkedIn-connection network plus past clients plus friends, this is achievable in 4–6 hours. Expect ~15% reply rate, ~3% "yes, let me introduce you to X," ~1% "yes, I have a project." That is 1–5 referred conversations and 1–5 real project leads from week one, which is more pipeline than the cold list can produce in a month.

Channel 2 — In-person + local groups

The second-most-valuable hour Brian can spend this month is at a room full of Charlotte business owners. Specifically:

  • Charlotte Regional Business Alliance / Charlotte Chamber events. Pick the two highest-density-of-buyer events per month. Bring matte-black business cards. Lead with the City of Charlotte case study within the first 30 seconds.
  • BNI chapters. Visit three in the first 60 days as a guest. Join the one with the highest concentration of $5M+ owner-operators. The "tech consultant" seat is usually open or weak in most chapters.
  • Charlotte Founders Slack / Queen City Mavericks / local Discord and Slack communities. Be useful for two weeks before pitching anything. Answer technical questions in public. Inbound DMs follow.
  • Warm intros via existing portfolio clients. The City of Charlotte engagement produced relationships with the CTO's office — request 2 named introductions. Stancil Services owner can refer one peer. Each portfolio client gets a structured ask.
  • Industry-specific local events. Charlotte Apartment Association (property management), Hood Hargett Breakfast Club, REBIC (real estate), local AGC chapter (construction). Pick one per month, attend twice before pitching.

Target: 3–4 in-person conversations per week with a fitting buyer. From that, expect 1 discovery call booked per week by week 4.

Channel 3 — Content + free tools

Content is the moat. It does not produce month-one revenue on its own, but it changes the conversion rate on every other channel from week 2 onward. A prospect who's seen one of Brian's LinkedIn posts, used the Cost Calculator, or read a case study is 5–10x more likely to reply to a cold DM and 2–3x more likely to convert a discovery call.

Free tools (highest-yield inbound asset)

One free tool on ascendsystems.ai converts cold visitors at 5–15% (vs. 0.5% for a generic "contact us"). Build order:

  1. Manual Process Cost Calculator (build first, this week). 5 inputs: hours/week on spreadsheet work, # of employees doing it, average hourly fully-loaded cost, error rate, customer-facing impact. Output: annual $ wasted + emailed PDF report + CTA "get a free 30-min audit." Single highest-yield asset for our ICP.
  2. AI Readiness Scorecard. 10 yes/no questions. Outputs A/B/C/D grade + personalized PDF. Gated behind email.
  3. CRM Build vs. Buy Calculator. Salesforce / HubSpot 3-year TCO vs. custom-build cost. Pre-populated with Charlotte SMB benchmarks. Outputs which is cheaper at the buyer's scale plus breakpoint year.
  4. Charlotte SMB Tech Report (annual). Survey 30+ Charlotte SMB owners on their tech stack, publish anonymized findings. Becomes lead magnet, PR hook, and "reason to email every Chamber member."

Channel 4 — Targeted cold outreach

The full playbook lives in the companion outreach doc. Headline rules:

  • Quality over volume. 5 highly personalized DMs per day beats 50 templated ones. Reply rates of 1–3% are the industry norm on raw cold — we lift that by leading with named portfolio proof (City of Charlotte, Stancil) and trigger-based openers (recent hire, recent funding, expansion).
  • List source: Outscraper + Apollo enrichment. Pull from Google Maps via Outscraper using the filter recipe in the outreach playbook (rating ≥ 4.0, 50+ reviews, has website, verified — these proxy revenue and legitimacy better than any single field). Enrich top 200 with Apollo for decision-maker contact.
  • Two-channel sequence per target: LinkedIn connection with a Charlotte-specific opener, then a 4-touch email sequence over 21 days. Every touch references a specific, real piece of portfolio work — never a generic value prop.
  • Volume target: 20 new prospects per week, 100 active sequences at steady state. Combined with warm + in-person + content, this produces 1–2 discovery calls per week by week 4.

Channel 5 — Paid amplification (not cold acquisition)

What we DO with paid (~$300/mo cap)

  • Boost the LinkedIn posts that hit organically. $20–50 behind a post that already has 10+ likes from real Charlotte buyers. Compounds the trust signal.
  • Retarget visitors to the Cost Calculator + case studies. Anyone who lands on ascendsystems.ai gets a soft retarget on LinkedIn for 30 days — "saw the City of Charlotte case study?" — keeps Ascend top of mind through the buying cycle.

What we do NOT do

Google search ads, Meta lead-form ads, or LinkedIn message ads to cold audiences. Buyers in our ICP do not click search ads to find a CTO — they ask a peer. Paid cold acquisition for a high-ticket consultancy at zero brand is a way to spend money slowly.

Revisit paid cold acquisition when

  • We have closed 6+ engagements and have 3+ filmable or quotable case studies
  • We have a productized offer (e.g., the Discovery Sprint) with a documented conversion rate
  • The Cost Calculator converts cold traffic at 5%+ (verified, not assumed)
  • We have $2,000+/mo of free cash to allocate without it hurting

Pricing posture

We anchor with three packages so the conversation starts in the right zip code. We close most deals with custom, value-based proposals built on the anchor. The packages exist to make pricing legible on a website and a first call, not to limit what we sell.

TierPriceWhat it isWhen to sell it
Discovery Sprint $2,500 – $5,000 2-week engagement. Audit current ops + tech stack, identify the highest-ROI build, deliver a written roadmap with scope and pricing for the build. Default first engagement. Removes risk for the buyer. Profitable on its own and converts ~70% into a Build engagement.
Build Engagement $10,000 – $50,000+ Fixed-scope build of a defined system: custom CRM, ops platform, internal dashboard, AI integration, customer portal. 4–12 weeks. Right after a successful Discovery Sprint, or when a buyer arrives with a clear, scoped need.
Fractional CTO / Operating Retainer $2,000 – $8,000 / mo Ongoing: 1–2 days per week. Strategy + hands-on engineering + vendor management + small-build velocity. 6-month minimum. After a Build engagement, or with a fast-growing company that needs a permanent technical seat without the headcount.

Pricing principles

  • Never quote on the first call. Pricing happens in a proposal, after we understand the value. The package anchors are conversational, not contractual.
  • Value-based, not hourly. Hourly billing caps the upside on the work we are best at. We sell outcomes.
  • Always include a stop point. Fixed-scope, fixed-price, fixed-deadline. The buyer needs to know what they are buying.
  • Reference the comparison. "An in-house hire would cost $200k; the big consultancy minimum is six figures; we will deliver this for $32k." The number lands differently when it is anchored.
  • Discounts only for case-study rights. A 10–15% reduction is fine in exchange for a filmed testimonial and a named public case study. Never discount to close.

Kill criteria and pivot triggers

Day 30 — kill or continue check

The strategy is working if by day 30 we have at least two discovery calls completed and one proposal sent. If we have neither, the problem is not the offer — it is volume. Triple the outbound and double the in-person events for the next 30 days before changing anything else.

Day 60 — pivot trigger

If by day 60 we have not closed at least one paid engagement (Discovery Sprint or Build), something is structurally wrong. Likely candidates: (1) the ICP definition is too broad and we are pitching the wrong buyer; (2) the Discovery Sprint price is wrong — either too high to be a no-brainer or too low to be taken seriously; (3) the proof is not landing — the case studies are not specific enough about outcomes. Run a positioning audit with three trusted Charlotte operators and pivot one variable, not all three.

Day 90 — full review

By day 90 we expect 3+ closed engagements and $25k–$75k of recognized revenue. If we are inside that band, double down: hire a VA for outbound, raise prices on the next round, and start the retainer motion. If we are below $10k, we have learned that the consultancy thesis is wrong for our current bandwidth — at that point, the right move is to pause Ascend's external marketing and rebuild the bottom of the funnel around a productized offering (e.g., a $497/mo "Charlotte SMB ops audit subscription") for 30 days before resuming consulting sales.

Permanent kill

If by day 180 we cannot show $75k+ of closed revenue and a pipeline of 10+ qualified conversations, Ascend is not pulling its weight against Brian's other portfolio bets. Wind down the marketing motion, keep the brand alive as the umbrella for individual client engagements, and reallocate the 5–10 hours per week to a higher-leverage portfolio asset.

What we are not doing

The plan is as much about subtraction as addition. The following are explicitly off the table for the next 90 days:

  • Paid ads. Wrong shape for the stage. Revisit at day 180+ once we have case study volume.
  • Vertical specialization. Tempting but premature. We learn which vertical pulls hardest from real closed deals, not from a guess made in week one.
  • Productizing too early. No fixed-scope "AI Audit" SKUs sold off the website. We sell custom engagements first; productize the patterns later when we have seen them three times.
  • Hiring before revenue. No subcontractors, no junior consultants, no agency partnerships in 90 days. Brian does the work. The work is the moat.
  • SEO at scale. The directory-site playbook does not apply here. One excellent technical blog post per week beats 100 thin programmatic pages.
  • National positioning. The website, the messaging, the outbound — all Charlotte-specific. "Tech consultant" is generic; "Charlotte tech consultant" wins searches and rooms.
  • The old AI phone-answering positioning. Dead. Removed from the brand, removed from the site, removed from the elevator pitch. Anything in older docs that mentions "Every call, handled" as the core offer is archive material.

The one thing

If only one sentence of this document survives the next 90 days, it is this: Brian Mangum has shipped real software for the City of Charlotte and for a real Charlotte business owner — and he is the one in the room. Every channel, every piece of content, every proposal is a vehicle for delivering that fact to the right buyer at the right moment. Everything else is logistics.
