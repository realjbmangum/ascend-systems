Ascend Systems — Design Critique

A buyer-personas-first walkthrough of ascendsystems.ai, scored as the first impression for cold and warm outreach starting Week 2. The verdict: visually competent, strategically incoherent — the home page sells a fake agency while the rest of the site tells the real (and better) story.

TL;DR + overall UX score

OVERALL UX SCORE — 58 / 100

The craft is here — clean type, restrained palette, well-paced sections, no AI-slop gradients. The strategy is broken. The home page invents a senior team, fabricates three named testimonials, and stamps fake hero metrics (47 engagements, $18M, 4.9 NPS, 11yr avg engineer tenure) onto what every other page admits is a one-person studio. A 25-person law firm COO will smell the inflation in under five seconds. A Charlotte HVAC owner-operator will bounce when the FAQ quotes $80K–$400K engagements. Fix the founder lie, ship the founder photo, and this is a 78. Don't, and the cold outreach campaign is pouring buyers into a credibility trap.

Strengths

Calling out what is actually working — these are keepers, not areas to overhaul.

  • Restrained, intentional brand system. Burnt orange #C45A2C against deep charcoal #1C1C1E on off-white reads like a small premium studio, not a SaaS landing-page generator. JetBrains Mono for eyebrows + numerics, Inter for body — it's a deliberate type-pairing, not the usual Inter-everywhere shortcut.
  • No AI-slop gradient hero. The hero uses a photographic mountain background under a charcoal overlay. No purple-to-blue gradient mesh, no glassmorphic cards, no glow effects. Already past the lowest bar most consultancy sites flunk.
  • Portfolio is the real moat. Eight live products with status pills, real metrics ("296 stores, 683 visitors/mo"), live "View live" buttons. Brian's portfolio is doing the work three fake testimonials are trying (and failing) to do on the home page.
  • The /services page reads like a person wrote it. "Your team copies quote info from email into a spreadsheet, then re-keys it into QuickBooks" — that is the voice of someone who has actually been in a Charlotte SMB back office. This is the page that converts.
  • The cost calculator is a real lead magnet. Four well-explained inputs ("$45–$75 is typical for ops staff in Charlotte"), localized to the buyer, no email-walled gate, an honest result-card with a band ("Light automation") and a payback range. This is the asset to lead with.
  • Process section is correctly understated. Discovery / Build / Launch / Support — four steps, no "agile pods at scale" vocabulary. P1 will respect this.
  • Contact form is right-sized. Five fields, budget range as a select (frames cost expectations early), one-business-day promise. Doesn't try to be clever.

Anti-pattern flags

Generic-AI-consultancy tropes audit. Format: present = found on the site, absent = good, you avoided it.

  • Present — fabricated testimonials with stock-sounding names. See issue #1. Worst offender on the site.
  • Present — round-number trust stats with no source. "47 engagements · $18M · 4.9 NPS" — classic AI-slop hero pattern. See issue #2.
  • Present — "trusted by" marquee with 4 logos doubled to look like 8. TrustedBySection.tsx sets const row = [...clients, ...clients]. Two of the four are text-only ("Carolina Masonry", "SuiteManager") because there are no logos. A sophisticated buyer will spot the duplication. Either ship 8 real logos, or kill the marquee and replace with a single sentence: "Recent clients include the City of Charlotte and Stancil Services."
  • Present — vague "senior team" / "staff-level engineers" copy on /home. "We're generalists with a narrow definition of quality. Every engagement is staffed with staff-level engineers and a product lead who has shipped the exact thing you're asking for, before." This is the LinkedIn-bot voice. The /services page (real voice) doesn't do this.
  • Present — hero copy is generic. "Software that moves legacy businesses forward" could be any of 200 consultancies. The Services-page hero "Your business has a technology problem. We fix it." is sharper, more specific, more Brian.
  • Absent — no stock photo people. Good.
  • Absent — no purple-blue gradient hero, no glassmorphic cards, no "AI-powered" stamp. Good.
  • Absent — no fake metrics on the case studies. Portfolio metrics are real and verifiable on the live links. Good.
  • Absent — no chatbot or "Talk to AI" widget. Good — would be self-parody on a "we build AI that works" site.
  • Absent — no nav with 9 items. Four items, clean. Good.

Net: the visual craft avoids most AI-slop tells. The copy is where the slop hides — testimonials, hero stats, marquee, and generic "senior team" language. Five surgical deletes would clean this up.

The cost calculator — persona-tested verdict

This shipped today. It's the single best-designed page on the site, and the most strategically important asset for the Week 2 outreach push. It deserves its own pass.

What it gets right

  • The hero promise is honest and specific. "Four numbers, one honest answer. We will email you a full breakdown plus a recommended next step — no sales pitch." That's a calibrated commitment, not a hand-wave.
  • The four-input model is well-chosen. Hours/week, hourly cost, error rate, customer impact — each is something a buyer can answer in under 30 seconds. Field hints localize the answer ("$45–$75 is typical for ops staff in Charlotte" — directly speaks to P1).
  • The result card is structured around money first, recommendation second, build investment third. A buyer sees the annual cost in 48pt mono, then the breakdown, then a "Light automation"-style band, then the build range. That's a textbook commerce funnel hierarchy.
  • The "$ build range / payback months" is the closer. It is genuinely rare for a Charlotte-area consultancy to show price ranges. This builds trust on a page where trust is the whole product.
  • Form mechanics are clean. Inline errors, no required-field walls, optional process-description that promises a smarter email if filled. Submit copy: "Show me the number" — strong, deferential, no exclamation marks.

What's broken

  • Zero discoverability. Already flagged as issue #5. This must be the first fix. If the cost calculator isn't in the nav before Monday's first outreach send, it doesn't exist.
  • No social proof on the page itself. P2 (law-firm COO) will scroll to the form, hesitate, and want to see "what does the email actually look like?" or "who has used this?" before handing over a work email. Add one inline example: a small "Real example: a 25-person ops team in Charlotte found $148K/yr in manual-work cost — and the build paid back in 4 months." Stub it as illustrative if no real one exists yet, but mark it so.
  • The "email gate" is invisible until the bottom. The Name / Work email / Company fields appear below a horizontal rule labeled "Where to send the report." P3 (warm collaborator) will be fine, but a cold-traffic P1 may bounce when they hit the email field because the page didn't telegraph it would email them. Surface it in the hero: "We'll show you the number on screen and email you the breakdown — your call whether you want the audit."
  • Submit button doesn't differentiate from /contact. Same orange, same shape, same weight. The result page also uses the orange-glow callout style that the /home services CTA uses. Visually, the calculator is doing the same job as the contact CTA — but it's a fundamentally different intent (information vs. commitment). Consider a secondary visual treatment for the result-card CTA so it feels like a follow-up, not a duplicate ask.
  • No back-out story for the "no recommended build" case. If the calculation lands in build_band: 'diy', the result page still shows "Want a free 30-minute audit?" — which contradicts the recommendation. Suppress or rewrite the audit CTA when the band is DIY: "Looks like a no-code fix — here are three tools that'll handle it" with affiliate-or-direct links.

Persona verdicts on the calculator

P1 (HVAC owner-operator): "Wait, this actually shows me a number? OK. I'd fill this out — but I'd hesitate at the email field if I hadn't been told it was coming."
P2 (law-firm COO): "Smart instrument. But who else has used this? Show me the methodology before I trust the number."
P3 (warm reactivation): "Brian built a calculator. Of course he did. I'd send this to my ops director."

Verdict: the page is a 9/10 craft, 6/10 distribution. Ship discoverability first, social proof second, no-build-case fallback third.

What I'd ship this week

Three changes Brian can ship in roughly 1–2 hours each that move the conversion needle the most. In ranked order:

1. Delete the fabricated testimonials section. (45 minutes)

File: src/components/TestimonialsSection.tsx. Remove from src/pages/Home.tsx imports. Don't replace it — leave a clean transition from the case-studies section directly to the FAQ. Empty space is better than fake people. If a real client (Stancil, Carolina Masonry, City of Charlotte) will sign off on a single quote, lead with that one quote and a real name + role. One is more credible than three fake ones. This is the highest-impact change on the site — it is the only change that removes a verifiable lie.

2. Add the cost calculator to the main nav + replace one home-page hero CTA. (30 minutes)

File: src/components/Nav.tsx. Add { to: '/tools/cost-calculator', label: 'Free Tools' } between Portfolio and About. File: src/components/HeroSection.tsx. Replace the secondary "See our work" CTA with <a href="/tools/cost-calculator">Calculate your manual-work cost →</a>. The primary "Book a discovery call" stays — but you now have a low-friction "tire-kicker" path that captures the 80% of cold traffic that isn't ready to book a call. The calculator is the lead magnet; the nav and hero are the megaphone.

3. Replace the hero stats row with three Brian can defend. (20 minutes)

File: src/components/HeroSection.tsx. Replace the four-stat HeroStatsComponent with three real ones: "10+ products shipped" · "Charlotte, NC, est. 2024" · "Founder-built". Or — and this is what I'd actually do — delete the stats row entirely. The hero copy is strong enough alone, and the home page is over-engineered. The 72px top margin of HeroStatsComponent is doing more credibility-damage than the four numbers are doing credibility-building. Less is more honest here.

That's it. Three files touched, ~90 minutes of work, and the home page goes from a 4/10 to a 7/10 for both buyer personas. None of these require new content, new photos, or new strategy. The actual sales engine of the site (Services + Portfolio + Calculator + Contact) is already good — these three fixes just stop the home page from sabotaging it.

One more thing — the "BM" placeholder on /about

This isn't in the top three because it's mid-week work (needs a photo), but it's the single highest-leverage permanent upgrade. The whole pitch is "you get the person." A monogram block where the photo should be is the visual equivalent of "placeholder text here" in production copy. Even a phone-camera photo in window light, cropped square, in front of a neutral wall, would be a step-function improvement. The site is otherwise within an inch of "credible owner-operator" — the missing photo is the difference between an inch and a mile.
