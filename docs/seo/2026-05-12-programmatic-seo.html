Ascend Systems — Programmatic SEO Plan

A template-driven page strategy designed for a consultancy, not a directory. Five overlapping page sets built on the React Router stack already in production — calibrated for 5–10 hours of marketing bandwidth per week and engineered to compound, not flood.

Charlotte NCSolo consultancyBrand-new domainPrepared 2026-05-12

TL;DR — what we ship, in this order

Five template sets, ~40 published pages over 12 weeks, built on the existing React Router + static TypeScript data pattern. Total estimated build: 50–70 hours of Brian's time spread across the quarter. No 1,000-page directory dumps. Quality threshold per page: 500+ unique words, one real artifact (case study reference, working calculator, screenshot, or architecture diagram), and content that only Ascend can write.

The five sets, in launch order

  1. /services/[slug] — 5 service pillar pages. Explode the existing single /services route. Effort: 8–10 hours. Cumulative monthly traffic estimate at month 6: 80–200 sessions est, needs verification via GSC. The foundation every other set links into.
  2. /charlotte/[service-slug] — 5 geo+service pages. Effort: 10–12 hours. Month-6 estimate: 150–400 sessions. Highest-intent buyer queries in Ascend's footprint.
  3. /tools/[tool-slug] — 4 free interactive tools (Cost Calc is shipped; add 3 more). Effort: 20–30 hours. Month-6 estimate: 200–500 sessions + the highest conversion rate of any page type (5–15% to email capture).
  4. /vs/[alternative-slug] — 6 comparison pages. Effort: 12–15 hours. Month-6 estimate: 100–300 sessions of the most commercial-intent traffic on the site.
  5. /industries/[slug] — 5 ICP-aligned industry pages (property management, law firms, multi-location services, trades, healthcare). Effort: 10–15 hours. Month-6 estimate: 80–200 sessions, but the highest sales-cycle compression on close.

Top priority keyword (the one to win first)

"custom CRM development charlotte" — high commercial intent, owned by directory aggregators with thin content, no Charlotte solo operator currently ranks. Win this with /charlotte/custom-crm-development + the underlying /services/custom-crm hub + the /vs/custom-crm-vs-salesforce comparison + the Cost Calculator tool. Four pages, one cluster, all pointing at one query family.

What we are explicitly NOT doing

  • No /charlotte-[industry]-software hyper-local matrix (e.g., "Charlotte HVAC software"). That's a directory play, and the SERP is owned by Jobber, Housecall Pro, ServiceTitan with massive budgets. Cannot win.
  • No /learn/[topic] educational glossary. SERP loses to Clio, Salesforce, Clutch.
  • No "Charlotte [city neighborhood] software" suburb permutations. Zero search volume; pure thin-content risk.

Methodology — how the five were chosen

This is a consultancy, not a marketplace. The programmatic-SEO playbook from the directory world (10,000 city pages, thin variable swaps) does not transfer. A consultancy lives or dies by 30–50 high-intent visitors per month who fit a narrow ICP. The shape of the asset has to match that economics.

Every candidate template set was scored on four dimensions, on a 1–5 scale, with the lowest score acting as the gate:

DimensionWhat it measuresWhy it's a hard gate
Buyer intent How close the searcher is to signing a check Informational traffic doesn't fund a consultancy. Commercial-intent traffic does.
Ranking feasibility SERP analysis: who ranks now, domain authority, content depth, geo modifier presence A brand-new domain cannot beat Salesforce, Clio, or Clutch on head terms. Pick fights we can win.
Content sustainability Can every page support 500+ words of genuinely unique content without padding? Thin-content penalty is the #1 risk of programmatic SEO. If we can't fill the page honestly, don't build it.
Stack feasibility Builds cleanly on React Router 7 + static TS data + Vite SSR/prerender Brian has 5–10 hrs/wk. If a template requires custom backend or external CMS, it doesn't ship.

Why /case-studies/[type] was deferred (not killed)

The existing /portfolio/:slug route already serves this need. Splitting case studies into a separate template set adds URL fragmentation and internal-linking complexity without adding ranking surface area — search intent for "Charlotte CRM case study" is approximately zero. The right move is to deepen the existing portfolio pages (richer write-ups, screenshots, architecture diagrams, named-client quotes) and use them as the proof-cited from the five template sets. Treat them as supporting infrastructure, not a programmatic surface.

Why /learn/[topic] was killed

SERP inspection on five informational queries — "what is a custom CRM," "what is a fractional CTO," "AI integration explained," "build vs buy software," "how much does a CRM cost" — shows the top 10 dominated by Clio (DA 78), Salesforce (DA 91), Clutch (DA 83), HubSpot (DA 93), and a tail of $5M-content-budget SaaS blogs. A brand-new DA-1 consultancy domain has no realistic path to top 10 inside 12 months. The CAC of an informational visitor is high; the volume we'd capture (10–30/mo per page, generously) doesn't repay the writing cost.

Why /charlotte-[business-type]-software was killed

SERP for "Charlotte HVAC software" is owned by national field-service-management SaaS — Jobber, Housecall Pro, ServiceTitan, Service Fusion. These vendors run $10M+/yr content programs and target the exact same buyer Ascend wants. Even if we ranked, the buyer who arrives is shopping for SaaS, not custom development — wrong intent for our offer. And the page can't honestly support 500+ unique words per (Charlotte × industry) cell without padding. Triple thin-content risk. Skip.

SERP analysis notes — what the top 10 actually look like

"custom CRM development charlotte"

SERP composition: 2 directory aggregators (Clutch, GoodFirms), 1 generic SaaS landing page (CustomCRMPros), 2 Charlotte-area firms with weak product proof (Pure Technology Consulting, Accessible Computing), 1 large agency page (Progatix), and rounded out by Salesforce consulting directory listings. The gap: no result on page 1 actually shows a Charlotte solo operator with a shipped CRM artifact. Ascend's wedge — the Stancil Services multi-tenant ops platform — is unmatched here. Win condition: a single 1,500-word page anchored on the Stancil case study with screenshots, an architecture diagram, and a clear pricing anchor. (Clutch CRM Charlotte, CustomCRMPros)

"Charlotte fractional CTO"

SERP composition: SERP is currently dominated by fractional CFO results, not CTO — Google misreads the query for the higher-volume CFO term. The actual CTO results that do appear: Bry AI / Bryan Dennstedt, TechCXO, Dualboot Partners' CTO page, and a handful of out-of-market national fractional firms. The gap: Bry AI and TechCXO are advisory-shop oriented; no Charlotte page exists that says "fractional CTO who ships code." That's Ascend's wedge. Win condition: page explicitly differentiates from advisory-only fractional CTOs by showing shipped artifacts (City of Charlotte EV Dashboard, Stancil). The CFO-vs-CTO ambiguity in the SERP is also an opportunity — a well-optimized page can claim disambiguation traffic.

"Charlotte AI consultant for small business"

SERP composition: Holistic Consulting Technologies (Davidson), Stonehill Innovation, Agentic Arc AI, Springman Consulting, Flux Form, Ademero, plus Clutch's AI directory. The gap: every page reads like generic agency copy — "we help Charlotte businesses with AI." None show a working AI product the buyer can poke at. Ascend has the LoveNotes AI message generator, Heirloom AI photo restoration, the Fanvue platform, and AI features baked into the EV Dashboard — actual deployed AI artifacts. Win condition: lead the page with one live AI feature the visitor can interact with on Ascend's own portfolio products.

"custom CRM vs Salesforce"

SERP composition: Salesforce itself ranks for the term (cleverly — they have a Custom CRM marketing page), then content-marketing blog posts from Getcha, EKFrazo, Venue, Pretius, 4Degrees. All informational, none with an interactive cost-comparison tool, all hedged ("it depends"). The gap: no page commits to a real ROI calculator with hard numbers. If Ascend ships /vs/custom-crm-vs-salesforce with an embedded calculator that takes 5 inputs and produces a 3-year TCO comparison, it's the only utility result on the page. Win condition: the page becomes a tool, not a blog post.

"custom software for property management companies"

SERP composition: total SaaS-vendor lock-out. Buildium, Propertyware, Yardi, Rent Manager, Rentvine, Inhabit — the entire page is property management SaaS. Glide is the lone "build custom" alternative. The gap: the keyword has wrong intent — visitors are evaluating SaaS, not contracting custom. We'd need to capture a long-tail variant: "custom property management software when Yardi doesn't fit" or "custom integrations for property management companies". This is why Industries is a P2 — high difficulty, but valuable as a sales-call asset for the buyers we do reach.

Data source

A single TypeScript file src/data/services.ts exporting a typed array. The page renders from that data plus per-slug MDX (or just JSX-embedded long-form prose) for the unique narrative section. Content blocks rendered (per page)

  1. Hero — H1 + 1-sentence outcome promise + primary CTA
  2. "The problem" — 200–300 words, Charlotte-SMB-specific, no boilerplate
  3. "How an engagement runs" — 4–6 steps, concrete, with timeline
  4. "Proof" — one named portfolio project rendered inline with screenshot
  5. Pricing anchor — Discovery Sprint price, typical Build range, Fractional rate
  6. FAQ — 5–7 questions, each answered in 80–150 unique words (FAQPage schema)
  7. Related services + related tools — internal linking hub
  8. Footer CTA — Discovery Sprint or Cost Calculator

Word count target

1,200–1,800 unique words per page. Hard floor: 1,000. Anything thinner gets the page held back until written properly.

React Router pattern

<Route path="/services" element={<Services />} />
<Route path="/services/:slug" element={<ServiceDetail />} />

// src/pages/services/ServiceDetail.tsx
import { useParams } from 'react-router-dom';
import { services } from '@/data/services';

export default function ServiceDetail() {
  const { slug } = useParams();
  const service = services.find((s) => s.slug === slug);
  if (!service) return <NotFound />;
  return <ServiceTemplate service={service} />;
}

Since the stack is Vite SPA, set up vite-plugin-ssg or react-snap to prerender each /services/[slug] to a static HTML file at build time. Without prerendering, Google can crawl but the indexation rate on CSR routes is materially worse — measured commonly at 60–70% vs. 95%+ for prerendered. This is the single most important technical-SEO call for the entire plan; the parallel technical-SEO audit should land on the same answer.

Launch sequence

Ship all 5 pages in week 1. They are mostly already written content (the existing /services page) just decomposed and deepened. This is the lowest-effort, highest-leverage set in the plan.

Template set 2 — /charlotte/[service-slug]

P0 · ship weeks 2–4

The geo-anchored variant of Set 1. These are the pages that win the buyer typing "Charlotte custom CRM" into Google at 9pm after a long day fighting with a spreadsheet. Highest commercial intent in the entire plan.

URL pattern

Why this isn't a duplicate of Set 1

Critical distinction — these pages must have materially different content from /services/[slug], or they get folded together by Google as duplicate content. The Charlotte pages are organized around local proof and local context:

  • Lead with named Charlotte clients (City of Charlotte, Stancil Services)
  • Reference Charlotte SMB pricing context (e.g., "what a $5M Charlotte business typically pays")
  • Cite local SERP context (Designli, Bry AI, Dualboot named explicitly with why the visitor might be choosing among them)
  • Charlotte-specific FAQ ("can you meet at my office in South End?", "do you work with Charlotte property management companies?")
  • NAP block (name, address, phone) for local-business schema

Why tools rank so well

SERP analysis on "build vs buy software calculator" shows SaaSFractionalCPO, Brilworks, and Ideanote ranking purely because they shipped an actual interactive calculator. Most "calculator" SERPs are still 70% blog posts because writers default to writing essays instead of building tools. Each tool is a React component with controlled inputs, instant computed output, and an "email me the full report" gate that drops the lead into the existing Ascend CRM. The honesty constraint

Comparison pages have a famous failure mode: writing one-sided propaganda that no one trusts. Google ranks balanced comparison pages much higher than biased ones; buyers convert from balanced pages much higher than biased ones. Every /vs/ page must include a clear "when you should pick the other option" section. Salesforce is right for some buyers. So is Bubble. Saying so builds the trust that makes the "when you should pick Ascend" section land.

Template set 5 — /industries/[slug]

P2 · ship weeks 8–12

The lowest-priority of the five but a high-leverage sales-call asset. These pages don't have to win Google to pay off — they have to exist so that when a property-management buyer asks "have you worked with property managers?" Brian can reply with a link, not a pitch. Any organic traffic is a bonus.

URL pattern

/industries/property-management, /industries/law-firms, /industries/multi-location-services, /industries/trades-and-contractors, /industries/healthcare-and-dental-groups

The ranking reality

SERP analysis on "custom software for property management companies" shows page 1 is entirely owned by property-management SaaS vendors (Buildium, Yardi, Propertyware). Same for "AI for law firms" (Clio, Spellbook, Harvey). We will not rank for the head term. What we can rank for: long-tail intent variations the SaaS vendors don't target, like "custom integrations for Buildium" or "AI document review for solo law firms."

Why we still build them

Three reasons, in order of value:

  1. Sales-cycle compression. When a property-management buyer arrives via warm intro or an event, having /industries/property-management to send before the discovery call shortens the sales cycle by 1–2 weeks.
  2. Long-tail capture. 10–30 sessions per month per page on niche, high-intent variations is enough to produce one quality lead per quarter per industry.
  3. Internal linking destination. Every other template set links here naturally. Builds topical authority for the broader domain.

Thin-content risk register

Programmatic SEO penalizes the careless. The five risks below are the ones that actually end programmatic experiments, ordered by probability for Ascend's plan.

Risk 1 — Duplicate content between /services/[slug] and /charlotte/[service-slug]

Probability: High if not policed. Impact: Google folds the two pages, only ranks one. Mitigation: Different content templates entirely (Set 1 organized by methodology, Set 2 organized by local proof + competitor context + meeting options). Different headings, different example projects, different CTA copy. Run a Copyscape or simple Jaccard-similarity check between each pair before publishing — target <40% overlap by sentence.

Risk 2 — Generic FAQ blocks that repeat across pages

Probability: High. The lazy default is to copy 3 generic FAQs across every page ("how long does this take?", "how much does it cost?"). Impact: FAQPage schema gets ignored, page perceived as templated. Mitigation: Every FAQ block is hand-written per page. Minimum 5 questions per page, minimum 80 words per answer, minimum 50% of questions unique to that page (not appearing on any other page).

Risk 3 — Comparison pages with no honest "pick the other option" section

Probability: Medium. Impact: Google's quality raters explicitly flag biased comparison content. Comparison pages with no concession to the alternative rank poorly and convert poorly. Mitigation: Required content block. PR checklist item before any /vs/ page ships.

Risk 4 — Industry pages without real industry-specific content

Probability: Medium. Easy to write generic "we help property managers" copy with no actual property-management expertise on the page. Impact: Thin-content downgrade, low rankings, low conversion. Mitigation: Every industry page must name at least 3 specific SaaS incumbents in the space with accurate descriptions, and at least 3 specific operational pain points. If Brian doesn't have the domain knowledge to write that page, hold it until he does.

Risk 5 — CSR routes not getting indexed

Probability: Medium-high without prerendering. Impact: Pages exist but never rank. Mitigation: vite-plugin-ssg or react-snap in the build pipeline. Each /services/[slug] and /charlotte/[slug] must produce a fully-rendered HTML file at build time, not a JS-only shell. This is a hard blocker — do not ship the strategy without it.

Operational discipline

Before any new :slug page goes live, it gets a one-page checklist review:

  • Unique title and meta description, 50–60 / 150–160 chars
  • One H1, hierarchical H2/H3
  • ≥1,000 unique words (verified against existing pages on the domain)
  • ≥3 internal links into the page and ≥3 out
  • Named portfolio reference or working calculator embedded
  • FAQPage schema, valid in Schema validator
  • Renders to static HTML in dist/ at build time
  • Listed in sitemap.xml

Measurement

Honesty: a brand-new domain does not rank on commercial-intent terms in month 1. Realistic timeline is 3–4 months to begin seeing indexation-level rankings (positions 30–70), 5–6 months for meaningful traffic (positions 10–30 on long-tail), and 9–12 months for top-10 positions on the priority terms. Anyone promising faster on a DA-1 site is selling something.

Month-3 review gate

By end of week 12: 24 pages live, 100% indexed, 3+ priority keywords showing in GSC (any position), 1+ email capture per week from a tool page. If indexation < 80%, the prerendering setup is broken — fix before adding pages. If 0 priority keywords visible, the content is too generic — revisit the keyword map before doubling down.

Month-6 review gate

The honest target: 200–500 organic sessions per month cumulative across all 24 pages, with 3–10% of tool-page sessions converting to email captures. That's 6–50 captured leads per month, of which 1–5 should be ICP-fit Charlotte SMBs. From a starting point of zero, that's a hummingly-good outcome for a consultancy site in month 6. Numbers above are estimates, needs verification against actual indexation curve.

Month-12 honesty check

If by month 12 we have not cracked top-10 on at least one of the four /charlotte/[service] pages, the assumption that local SERPs are winnable for Ascend's wedge was wrong. At that point the right move is to reallocate the SEO time into outbound and in-person — the marketing strategy already says these are higher-conversion channels in the early days. Programmatic SEO is a hedge, not the lead engine.

The one sentence

Build 24 honest pages across 5 templates organized around buyer intent, not 1,000 thin pages organized around variable swaps — and treat the Cost Calculator pattern as the engine, the Charlotte+service pages as the prize, and everything else as connective tissue that makes the first two rank.
