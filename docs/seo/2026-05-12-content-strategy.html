Twelve weeks of writing that turns shipped work into trust, trust into discovery calls, and discovery calls into the first six engagements. One piece a week. No filler. No hot takes. Every word anchored on real, named, in-Charlotte deployments.

TL;DR

The five hubs

  1. Custom software for the City of Charlotte and Charlotte SMBs — the case-study hub. Every piece here links back to the City of Charlotte EV Dashboard write-up and the Stancil Services ops platform write-up. This is the wedge.
  2. Custom CRM vs. off-the-shelf — the highest-commercial-intent hub. Buyers in our ICP search for Salesforce alternatives and HubSpot escape hatches every week. We answer in plain English with TCO numbers.
  3. AI for Charlotte SMBs (de-hyped) — the de-snake-oil hub. Owner-operators have been pitched fake AI by ten reps before they find us. We earn trust by being the one writeup that names actual integrations, costs, and limits.
  4. The solo-operator development model — the objection-handler hub. "Why would I hire one person instead of an agency?" turned into a series of pieces that pre-answer every doubt before the discovery call.
  5. Charlotte tech and SMB economy commentary — the local-authority hub. Lower priority, lower frequency. Earns local backlinks and signals geo-relevance for everything else.

Three pieces to ship in Week 1

  1. The City of Charlotte EV Dashboard case study — 2,500 words, screenshots, the architecture, the constraints, the public URL. Anchors the entire site. Highest-leverage single piece in the strategy.
  2. Custom CRM vs. Salesforce for a $5M Charlotte business — the actual 3-year math — commercial intent, links to the Cost Calculator, becomes the gravity well for the CRM hub.
  3. AI for a $10M services business: what actually works, what does not — kills the snake-oil framing on contact, positions Brian as the calm technical voice in a noisy market.

Realistic day-90 outcome

Twelve pieces of long-form content + ~36 LinkedIn posts + 1 promotional asset. Honest traffic estimate from new content at day 90: 100–500 monthly organic visitors, weighted heavily toward the case studies and the CRM-vs-Salesforce piece. Most of these visitors are tire-kickers. The real ROI of these 90 days is (a) Google starting to index and rank, with traction visible months 4–9, and (b) every cold DM, warm intro, and in-person conversation closing harder because the prospect has read or seen something before the call. Content is the moat, not the funnel.

Strategic frame

The competitive analysis surfaced one defensible wedge: verifiable, named, in-Charlotte shipped product. Bry AI has the YouTube flywheel. Designli has the review moat. Dualboot has the bench. None of them can point to a public-sector deployment a buyer can drive past, plus a private-sector ops platform whose operator will answer the phone. That asymmetry is the entire content strategy.

The content does three jobs, in this order of priority:

  1. Make every discovery call close harder. A prospect who has read the City of Charlotte case study before the call has 80% of the trust work done. The content's first job is to be the homework a serious buyer does before they pick up the phone.
  2. Earn search traffic on commercial-intent queries. Targeted slowly. The "custom CRM vs Salesforce" type queries take 6–9 months to rank for, but they convert at 5–10x informational queries when they do.
  3. Generate a LinkedIn distribution surface. Every long-form piece spawns 2–4 LinkedIn posts. LinkedIn is Brian's actual audience for the first 90 days. Search is the long game.

The constraint that drives everything else: Brian has 1.5–2.5 hours per week for writing. That budget yields exactly one well-researched long-form piece per week — no more. A plan that asks for two pieces a week is a plan that fails by week three. The discipline is one excellent piece, repurposed into LinkedIn assets that fill the rest of the week.

Why this sequence and not a different one

  • Weeks 1, 4, 10 are case studies. They are the highest-leverage pieces in the whole calendar, so they bracket the quarter. Week 1 sets the tone; Week 4 reinforces; Week 10 broadens to public-sector lessons.
  • Weeks 2 and 5 land the Custom CRM hub early. Custom CRM is the highest commercial intent of the five hubs. Search rankings take months to compound, so the pieces that need to rank earliest get published earliest.
  • Weeks 6, 8, 9 handle objections. By the second month, discovery calls are happening. The objection-handler pieces in the Solo-Operator hub directly pre-answer the doubts that come up live.
  • Week 12 is the linkable asset. The Charlotte SMB Tech Report is the only piece that requires outside input (a 30-respondent survey). Scheduling it at the end gives 8 weeks of lead time to collect responses while other pieces ship.

The City of Charlotte case study — full template

This is the single highest-leverage piece of content in the entire 90-day plan. The competitive analysis flagged it explicitly: no other Charlotte consultancy at this price tier has a public-sector deployment a prospect can verify in 30 seconds. The case study must be written as if a deeply technical buyer — say, a director of operations who used to be a developer — is reading it. If it stands up to that reader, it stands up to everyone below.

Structure — eight sections, ~2,500 words

#SectionWord countAnchor proof
1 Opening (the public URL) ~200 The live URL embedded, the screenshot, the one-paragraph "what this is" framing.
2 The problem ~300 Plain-English description of what the City needed and why their existing reporting was inadequate. No NDA-leaking specifics — just enough to make the constraints legible.
3 The constraints ~300 Five real constraints: public data, accessibility, budget, timeline, who would maintain it. List them, explain each.
4 Technical approach ~600 The actual stack (Cloudflare Pages + D1 + cron worker for ingestion + Astro frontend). One architecture diagram. Two short code or config snippets where they make the engineering decision concrete. This is the section that closes technical readers.
5 Screenshots walkthrough ~400 4–6 screenshots, each captioned with the design decision behind it. Treat them as the visual spine of the piece.
6 Results ~300 What the City uses it for now, uptime, response time, a named-person quote with permission, or a paraphrased outcome if not.
7 Lessons for private-sector buyers ~300 The bridge section. "If you're a $10M services business, here are the three things from this project that apply to you." Pivots the case study from interesting-story to relevant-pitch.
8 CTA + sibling links ~100 Discovery Sprint link, Stancil case study link, hub link, Cost Calculator.

Screenshots needed

  1. Full hero shot of the dashboard, high-resolution, on a clean background
  2. The data filters / controls panel close-up
  3. One map or chart view at full fidelity
  4. The mobile responsive view
  5. The accessibility/WCAG audit pass (Lighthouse or similar)
  6. The architecture diagram (draw clean in Excalidraw or Figma)

What to avoid

  • NDA-leakage paranoia turning the piece bland. The data is public, the URL is public, the stack is unremarkable. Treat it that way. Do not write the piece like a redacted document.
  • Heroics framing. "I built this in a weekend" is the wrong tone. The right tone is "modern tooling makes this kind of work approachable for a solo operator, and here's what that looks like."
  • Hand-waving on the engineering. One real architecture diagram and two real snippets. If the technical buyer doesn't believe a developer wrote this, the wedge collapses.

LinkedIn distribution playbook

Three posts per week, every week, all repurposed from the one long-form piece. LinkedIn is Brian's actual audience for the 90-day window. Search traffic compounds slowly; LinkedIn impressions compound from week 2 if the cadence holds.

Format rules

  • Carousels for visuals. Architecture diagrams, screenshots, TCO tables, before/after. Anything where the visual is the point.
  • Text-only for opinions. Contrarian takes, lessons, observations. Text-only posts have the highest engagement when the opening line earns the click-to-expand.
  • Single image for one strong visual. When you have exactly one screenshot worth showing, lead with it. Don't pad it into a carousel.
  • No native video for the first 90 days. Video has the highest production cost and the budget doesn't support it. Revisit at day 90.

Opening-line patterns that earn the expand-click

The first two lines of a LinkedIn post are everything. If they don't earn the expand, the rest of the post is dead. Use these patterns:

  • The headline number: "Over three years, a $5M Charlotte business pays Salesforce $90K. A custom build costs $35K once."
  • The contrarian flag: "I'm going to argue against the thing I sell for a minute."
  • The shipped-thing flag: "Here's a dashboard I built for the City of Charlotte that's running in production right now."
  • The blunt observation: "Most 'AI tools' pitched to Charlotte SMBs in 2026 are nonsense. Here's the four use cases that actually pay back."

What never gets posted

  • Generic "what AI means for business" thinkpieces with no specific number, screenshot, or named client
  • Hot takes on the consultancy industry that don't tie to a buyer decision
  • Reposts of someone else's content with a one-line "great point" caption
  • Anything that sounds like it was written by an AI prompt with "make this engaging"

Promotional pieces — the linkable assets

Two assets to build inside the 90-day window, plus one already-shipped asset to lean on. These are the pieces that other people link to and cite, which is the only durable way to earn ranking signals outside of search-intent content. They cost more than a long-form post to produce, but they keep paying back for years.

Asset 1 — The Charlotte SMB Tech Report 2026

Scope: Anonymized survey of 30+ Charlotte SMB owners ($1M–$50M revenue, owner-operator, any industry). 15 questions covering: current tech stack, monthly software spend, biggest operational pain, AI usage / attitudes, build vs. buy preferences, plans for the next 12 months.

Effort: ~12 hours over 6 weeks. Survey design (2 hr), distribution via Chamber + BNI + LinkedIn warm list (2 hr), follow-up nudging (2 hr), analysis (3 hr), write-up + design (3 hr).

Why it earns links: Original data is the most linkable asset type in content marketing. Local press (Charlotte Business Journal, Charlotte Inno, WCNC business section) covers original surveys of local businesses. Chambers and BNI chapters distribute reports about their members. Anyone writing about "Charlotte SMB technology" will cite the data once it exists.

Secondary use: Becomes a lead magnet on the site (gated by email), a reason to email every Chamber contact, and the basis for 4–6 LinkedIn posts that get distributed for months.

Publication target: Week 12. Survey opens Week 4.

Asset 2 — The CRM Build vs. Buy Calculator

Scope: Interactive on-site calculator. Inputs: team size, revenue, current CRM (Salesforce / HubSpot / GoHighLevel / spreadsheets), required integrations. Outputs: 3-year TCO for Salesforce, HubSpot, custom build, and the breakpoint year where custom becomes cheaper. Gated PDF version with personalized analysis.

Effort: ~8 hours of build time. Brian can build this himself in two evening sessions, but it counts against his content time, so plan a single Saturday afternoon.

Why it earns links: Calculators are the single most-linked asset type in B2B content. The Manual Process Cost Calculator already proves the format works on this site. People link to tools that produce a personalized number; they almost never link to a 1,500-word essay.

Secondary use: Anchors the entire Custom CRM hub. Every CRM-hub piece links to it. Becomes the primary CTA on the /custom-crm pillar page.

Publication target: Week 6 (so it's live to anchor the late-quarter CRM pieces).

Asset 3 (already shipped) — Manual Process Cost Calculator

Already live. Treat it as the existing linkable asset and link to it from every relevant piece (especially the CRM hub and the AI hub). When the Charlotte SMB Tech Report is published, include a callout in the report directing readers to the Cost Calculator for their own number. The two pieces should cross-promote.

Measurement

The strategy is honest about what compounds when. Tracking weekly keeps the loop tight; tracking the right metrics keeps it sane. The temptation will be to measure traffic on day 30 and panic. Don't. Day-90 traffic from new content for a site at zero organic authority is realistically in the 100–500 monthly visitors range, weighted toward branded queries. The lagging-but-real signal is GSC impressions, not sessions.

When ranking traction is realistically expected

  • Weeks 1–4: Indexing. Pages start showing in GSC for branded and very-long-tail queries. No commercial-intent rankings yet.
  • Weeks 5–10: Impressions climb. GSC will show pages ranking positions 20–60 on long-tail queries. Clicks still rare.
  • Weeks 11–18: First real rankings on long-tail commercial queries. The custom-CRM-vs-Salesforce piece is the most likely first winner.
  • Months 6–12: Pillar pages mature. Hub pages start ranking on broader queries. This is when monthly organic visitors realistically climb past 1,000.

The early signals that precede traffic

  1. GSC impressions trending up — usually visible by week 6. The single best leading indicator.
  2. LinkedIn engagement from Charlotte buyers specifically — not vanity reach, but real local profiles commenting. If the right people are reading the LinkedIn posts, the content is targeted right.
  3. Discovery calls where the prospect cites the content — "I read your City of Charlotte writeup" is worth ten LinkedIn impressions.
  4. Direct/branded search traffic — people Googling "ascend systems charlotte" instead of arriving via a search-intent query. Indicates word-of-mouth is starting.
  5. Inbound backlinks (without asking) — the rarest early signal but the most predictive. One unprompted link from a Charlotte business publication is worth months of organic-traffic patience.

Programmatic SEO / city-list pages That's a separate skill applied to directory sites. Wrong shape for a high-ticket consultancy. The other agent on this engagement is handling this lane.
Listicles ("Top 10 CRMs for Small Businesses") These are commodity content. They rank only with massive domain authority we don't have, and they don't differentiate. Brian's writeups need to feel like Brian wrote them, not like a content farm.
Personal-brand content ("My journey as a developer") The audience cares about their problem, not Brian's career. Personal content earns engagement on Twitter and erodes credibility on LinkedIn-for-buyers. Save it for the about page.
Trend-jacking ("What the new GPT release means for SMBs") News-cycle content has a half-life of 72 hours. Evergreen pieces that survive 2 years are 100x the ROI per writing hour.
Vague case studies without screenshots or numbers "We helped a client streamline operations" without specifics is worse than no case study at all. If we can't say the client's name, show a screenshot, or quote a number, the case study isn't ready to publish.

Operating principles for the 90 days

  1. Ship the case studies first. The City of Charlotte writeup is the highest-leverage single piece. If anything else gets cut, it cannot.
  2. One piece a week. Not two. The plan is built on Brian's actual bandwidth. Two pieces a week is the way it dies in month two.
  3. Every piece has a number, a screenshot, or a named client. The differentiation is verifiable proof. No abstractions, no hand-waving, no "industry research shows..."
  4. Every piece links to a case study. The case studies are gravity wells. Spokes that don't link back to a hub aren't pulling their weight.
  5. LinkedIn distribution is not optional. A long-form piece that doesn't get repurposed onto LinkedIn delivers maybe 20% of its potential ROI. The repurposing is the multiplier.
  6. The day-90 target is pipeline, not traffic. If 12 long-form pieces produce 0 organic visitors but every discovery call cites the content, the strategy worked. Traffic is the lagging indicator; trust is the leading one.
The cadence is the only thing that matters. One piece per week for 12 weeks, repurposed three ways onto LinkedIn, anchored on real shipped work, will out-compound a brilliant strategy executed sporadically. The content is the moat, but the moat only forms if the work keeps showing up.
